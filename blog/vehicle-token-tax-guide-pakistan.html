Vehicle Token Tax in Pakistan 2026

PakTaxCalc Team • April 7, 2026

Vehicle token tax becomes a headache when people mix too many vehicle charges into one conversation. Renewal tax, registration cost, transfer fees, customs, and purchase-side advance tax are not all the same thing. If your real question is "How much token tax should I expect at renewal time?" then the right place to start is section 234.

FBR's current rate card updated up to June 30, 2025 shows fixed section 234 amounts for private vehicles based on engine-capacity brackets, with separate figures for ATL and non-ATL users and a separate schedule where motor vehicle tax is collected in lump sum. That is the structure this guide explains.

What Is Vehicle Token Tax?

In everyday usage, people call a lot of different vehicle charges "token tax." For this guide, we are talking specifically about the fixed section 234 tax collected with motor vehicle tax renewal for common private passenger vehicles. That narrower scope matters because it keeps the answer useful.

If someone really needs customs duty, provincial registration, or purchase-side advance tax, that is a different page and often a different formula. For renewal planning, section 234 is the cleanest and most relevant starting point.

Current Section 234 Rates by Engine Capacity

The current FBR rate card shows the following annual section 234 private-vehicle amounts:

  • Up to 1000cc: Rs. 800 ATL, Rs. 1,600 non-ATL
  • 1001cc to 1199cc: Rs. 1,500 ATL, Rs. 3,000 non-ATL
  • 1200cc to 1299cc: Rs. 1,750 ATL, Rs. 3,500 non-ATL
  • 1300cc to 1499cc: Rs. 2,500 ATL, Rs. 5,000 non-ATL
  • 1500cc to 1599cc: Rs. 3,750 ATL, Rs. 7,500 non-ATL
  • 1600cc to 1999cc: Rs. 4,500 ATL, Rs. 9,000 non-ATL
  • 2000cc and above: Rs. 10,000 ATL, Rs. 20,000 non-ATL

These rates are the numbers many vehicle owners are actually trying to find when renewal time gets close.

Annual vs Lump-Sum Collection

One of the biggest reasons people misjudge token tax is that they use the annual table when the applicable case is actually lump-sum collection. The current rate card provides a separate lump-sum schedule, and the figures are significantly higher.

For example, the lump-sum table starts at Rs. 10,000 ATL and Rs. 20,000 non-ATL for up to 1000cc, and goes up to Rs. 120,000 ATL and Rs. 240,000 non-ATL for 2000cc and above. So mode is not a small checkbox. It changes the whole cost picture.

That is why the vehicle token tax calculator asks for collection mode explicitly.

Why ATL Status Matters So Much

Vehicle token tax is one of the clearest examples of how filer status shows up in real-life asset ownership. The spread between ATL and non-ATL in the current tables is large enough that the same car can feel meaningfully more expensive simply because the owner's tax status is different.

This is why many people only start caring about ATL after they compare renewal figures. The rate card turns a legal label into a real cash difference. Example 1: 1300cc to 1499cc Annual Renewal

Suppose a private vehicle falls in the 1300cc to 1499cc bracket. Under the current annual section 234 table, the tax is Rs. 2,500 for ATL and Rs. 5,000 for non-ATL. The engine bracket is the same, the renewal event is the same, but the owner's status doubles the tax.

That difference is why renewal planning should never ignore ATL. For some users, the tax-status issue is more important than the engine bracket itself.

Example 2: Higher Engine and Lump-Sum Case

Now consider a higher engine-capacity vehicle in a lump-sum collection scenario. The user may be expecting an annual-style figure, but the applicable amount could be dramatically larger. That is the kind of mistake that leads to last-minute stress and budget shock.

A good guide should make one thing clear: annual and lump-sum are not tiny variations of the same number. They are different cost shapes entirely.

How To Check the Right Bracket

Do not guess from model names or dealership language. Token tax planning works best when you use the actual engine-capacity bracket shown in the registration record or vehicle documents. A 1299cc versus 1300cc difference may sound small in conversation, but on the rate table it changes the amount.

If you are unsure, verify the engine size first and only then estimate the tax.

Common Mistakes People Make

  • Mixing renewal token tax with registration, transfer, or customs cost
  • Using the wrong engine bracket based on informal model naming
  • Using annual figures in a lump-sum case
  • Ignoring ATL status when it materially changes the number
  • Relying on old screenshots instead of current published rate-card figures
